Project Case Study

Autonomous Window Cleaning Robot

A step-by-step look at the design process behind a residential autonomous window-cleaning system, from initial problem definition to prototype testing and final presentation.

Project Overview

This project focused on designing a cost-effective automated window-cleaning system for residential use. The final system used a gantry-style motion platform, belt and pulley mechanism, stepper motor, Arduino control, limit switches, a squeegee system, and integrated water delivery.

Design Process

1. Define the Problem

The team was tasked with designing a solution to a real-world problem while working within a strict $150 budget. After researching existing automated window-cleaning systems, we found that most commercial solutions were designed for skyscrapers and large commercial buildings. Rather than competing with those systems, we shifted our focus toward residential homeowners, where window cleaning is often overlooked due to the cost and inconvenience of hiring professional services. In our area, residential window cleaning typically costs between $150 and $250 per visit.

Our objective was to develop a low-cost autonomous window-cleaning system that could be permanently installed on a residential window, reducing the need for repeated professional cleaning. The system needed to travel across the window, apply sufficient pressure through a squeegee to remove debris, and eventually integrate a water delivery system to improve cleaning performance. Throughout the project, we balanced performance, manufacturability, and cost while iteratively refining the design through multiple prototypes.

2. Ideate Motion Concepts

Early brainstorming explored several approaches for moving the cleaning mechanism across the window. Concepts included a CNC-style gantry system, suspended rope-and-sponge mechanisms, and other automated cleaning methods. After evaluating each concept for feasibility, complexity, and cost, we selected a belt-driven linear motion system powered by a stepper motor. This approach provided precise positioning while remaining within our $150 budget.

Once the motion system was selected, we identified the major engineering challenges that would guide the remainder of the project. The following design requirements became the foundation for each subsequent prototype:

  • Linear Motion: Design a belt-driven gantry system capable of moving smoothly and reliably across the full width of the window.
  • Complete Surface Coverage: Because the platform only traveled along one axis, the squeegee needed to span the entire height of the window to clean the surface in a single pass.
  • Water Delivery: Integrate a system capable of dispensing water immediately before the squeegee passed over the glass.
  • Consistent Contact Force: Maintain sufficient and uniform pressure between the squeegee and the window to effectively remove debris.
  • Motor Selection: Ensure the selected stepper motor could provide enough torque to move the cleaning assembly while maintaining smooth motion.
  • Structural Design: Develop a lightweight yet rigid frame that supported the motion system while remaining within budget.
  • Low-Cost Manufacturing: Keep the complete prototype under the project's $150 budget through careful component selection and 3D-printed parts.
  • Reliability: Create a system capable of repeating the cleaning cycle consistently using Arduino-based motor control and limit switches.

4. Prototype 2: Linear Motion System The second prototype focused on x-direction movement using a stepper motor, pulley, belt, track, and Arduino controller. Torque calculations were used to confirm that the motor could move the required load.
5. Prototype 3: Mounting and Frame Development The team developed a homemade window frame, mounted the gantry system, and wrote Arduino code for motion. This prototype exposed manufacturing and assembly issues, including frame sizing, drilling constraints, and delayed electrical components.
6. Prototype 4: Water Delivery and Squeegee Force The design expanded to include water delivery, a full-length squeegee, and an applied force mechanism. I worked on planning the water system, including fittings, tubing, nozzle behavior, and head-loss calculations.
7. Prototype 5: Integrated Final Prototype The final prototype combined the gantry, tubing, squeegee, window frame, electronics, and water delivery into one working system.
8. Final Presentation The final design cleaned the window back and forth, moved smoothly along the V-slot, changed direction reliably, and delivered enough water to clean the surface with minimal friction.

Engineering Calculations

Motor Motion

The team calculated the number of motor rotations required to move the platform across the full window length. For a 500 mm travel distance and a 16 mm pulley diameter, the system required approximately 9.95 rotations.

Fluid Delivery

The water system required estimating flow behavior through tubing and nozzle geometry. Head loss and spray velocity were considered to ensure enough water reached the window surface before the squeegee passed over it.

My Contributions

  • Led planning for later prototype iterations and water delivery integration.
  • Calculated flow requirements, nozzle behavior, and tubing head loss.
  • Helped connect the water delivery system to the gantry and cleaning motion.
  • Supported prototype iteration, design tradeoffs, and final presentation preparation.

What We Learned

The project required constant iteration. Parts arrived late, some 3D prints failed, and the final design changed significantly from the original concept. The biggest lesson was learning to plan for failure, make fast engineering decisions, and adapt the design without losing sight of the core function.

Result

The final prototype successfully demonstrated an automated cleaning motion with smooth gantry movement, water delivery, squeegee contact, and reliable direction changes.
