Project Case Study

Autonomous Window Cleaning Robot

A step-by-step look at the design and development of a low-cost residential window-cleaning system, from problem definition and concept selection through five prototypes, testing, iteration, and final presentation.

Project Overview

The team was tasked with designing a solution to a real-world problem while working within a strict $150 budget. After researching existing automated window-cleaning systems, we found that most commercial solutions were designed for skyscrapers and large commercial buildings.

Rather than competing with those systems, we shifted our focus toward residential homeowners, where window cleaning is often overlooked because of the cost and inconvenience of hiring professional services. In our area, residential window cleaning typically cost between $150 and $250 per visit.

Our objective was to develop a low-cost autonomous window-cleaning system that could be permanently installed on a residential window, reducing the need for repeated professional cleaning. The system needed to travel across the window, apply sufficient pressure through a squeegee to remove debris, and integrate a water-delivery system to improve cleaning performance.

Concept Development

Early brainstorming explored several approaches for moving the cleaning mechanism across the window. Concepts included a CNC-style gantry system, suspended rope-and-sponge mechanisms, and other automated cleaning methods.

After evaluating each concept for feasibility, complexity, reliability, and cost, we selected a belt-driven linear-motion system powered by a stepper motor. This approach provided controlled positioning while remaining feasible within the project budget.

Design Requirements

Once the motion system was selected, the following requirements guided every subsequent prototype:

Linear Motion

Develop a belt-driven gantry capable of moving smoothly and reliably across the full width of the window.

Complete Surface Coverage

Because the platform moved along one axis, the squeegee needed to span the full height of the window.

Water Delivery

Dispense water onto the glass immediately before the squeegee passed over the cleaning area.

Consistent Contact Force

Maintain sufficient and uniform pressure between the squeegee and the glass to remove debris.

Motor Capacity

Select a stepper motor with enough torque to move the complete cleaning assembly without stalling.

Structural Stability

Create a lightweight but rigid frame capable of supporting the motion, cleaning, and water-delivery systems.

Low-Cost Manufacturing

Keep the entire prototype below $150 through careful sourcing and the use of custom 3D-printed components.

Repeatable Operation

Use Arduino-based motor control and limit switches to repeat the cleaning cycle consistently.

Prototype 01

Cleaning Feasibility

Validating the squeegee concept and required contact force

Objective

The first prototype was designed to validate the cleaning concept before the team invested time and budget in a complete automated system. We tested whether a 3D-printed cleaning assembly paired with a rubber squeegee could effectively remove debris from a mirror.

Testing Method

Different weights were applied to the cleaning assembly to determine how much contact force was required to completely clean the mirror. The weight was increased incrementally, and the surface was evaluated after each cleaning pass.

Key Findings

  • Validated the use of a rubber squeegee as the primary cleaning method.
  • Found that approximately 543 grams of applied weight produced the strongest cleaning performance.
  • Confirmed that maintaining contact force would be a major requirement for future prototypes.

How It Informed Prototype 2

  • The motor needed to move the cleaning assembly while overcoming friction.
  • The structure needed to support the required contact force.
  • The motion mechanism needed to keep the squeegee stable during travel.

What I Learned

The project required constant iteration. Components arrived late, several 3D-printed parts failed or did not fit, and the final design changed significantly from the original concept.

The most important lesson was learning to plan for failure, create backup paths, begin rework immediately, and make fast engineering decisions without losing sight of the system's core requirements. The project strengthened my ability to adapt a design under budget, schedule, manufacturing, and integration constraints.
