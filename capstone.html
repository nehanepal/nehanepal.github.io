Project Case Study

Spacecraft Camera Housing

A Northrop Grumman-sponsored senior capstone project focused on designing a protective housing for off-the-shelf cameras used during spacecraft thermal vacuum testing.

Project Overview

Thermal chamber testing is used to evaluate spacecraft components under extreme environmental conditions. The goal of this project was to design a camera housing that would allow visual monitoring inside a chamber while protecting commercial camera hardware from temperatures ranging from −180°C to +130°C and vacuum pressures down to 10⁻⁴ torr.

Design Process

1. Problem Definition Define the need for reliable visual monitoring during spacecraft component testing without exposing camera hardware directly to extreme thermal vacuum conditions.
2. Requirement Development Translate sponsor needs into engineering requirements, including thermal survivability, vacuum compatibility, manufacturability, camera visibility, and structural integrity.
3. Research Research thermal expansion, vacuum-compatible materials, optical access, sealing methods, and heat transfer considerations for hardware operating in extreme environments.
4. Concept Generation Develop multiple housing concepts and evaluate tradeoffs related to geometry, manufacturability, thermal behavior, and camera accessibility.
5. CAD and Analysis Model housing concepts in SolidWorks and use simulation tools to evaluate structural and thermal behavior across expected operating conditions.
6. Prototype Planning Prepare a prototype-ready design, identify manufacturing constraints, and plan validation methods for thermal and vacuum performance.
7. Testing and Presentation Validate the design through analysis, prototype testing, and final presentation at the UCSB Capstone Fair.

My Role

  • Served as team lead for a five-person engineering team.
  • Coordinated project planning, sponsor communication, and technical deliverables.
  • Supported mechanical design decisions related to housing geometry, thermal constraints, and manufacturability.
  • Worked with Northrop Grumman engineers, UCSB faculty, and teaching staff to align design direction with sponsor requirements.

Engineering Focus Areas

  • Thermal design for −180°C to +130°C environments.
  • Vacuum compatibility down to 10⁻⁴ torr.
  • Material selection and thermal expansion considerations.
  • Structural integrity under testing conditions.
  • Camera visibility, mounting, and protection.

Result

The project developed a prototype architecture for a protective camera housing intended to support visual monitoring during spacecraft thermal vacuum testing. The work combined aerospace design constraints, mechanical design, thermal-fluid reasoning, CAD, analysis, and sponsor-driven engineering decision-making.
